# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Mixed audience with three overlapping jobs-to-be-done:

- **Hiring managers / recruiters** vetting a potential hire. They want a fast, confident answer to "can this person ship?" — they scan, they don't read.
- **Other developers** exploring work or getting inspired. They want signal about what this person finds interesting to build and how they think.
- **Clients / collaborators** evaluating a potential engagement. They want to trust that quality and follow-through come standard.

Context: they arrive from GitHub, a job listing, a tweet, or word of mouth. First impression is everything. They're assessing competence and taste simultaneously.

## Product Purpose

Personal portfolio, blog, and guestbook for Anish Shobith P S — software developer. The site exists to establish credibility, communicate personality, and convert interest into a conversation. Success looks like: a visitor who arrives uncertain and leaves with a clear read on who Anish is and why they'd want to work with him.

Secondary purpose: a living demonstration that Anish can design and ship interfaces — the site itself is a portfolio piece.

## Brand Personality

Refined, technical, deliberate.

Voice: dry wit, no fluff. The copy already says it — "interfaces, bots, and questionable automation scripts, mostly so I don't have to repeat myself." That sentence is the personality. Self-aware, precise, a little deadpan.

Emotional goal: visitors leave feeling *confident and slightly impressed* — not overwhelmed, not entertained, not inspired in a vague way. Quietly assured.

## Anti-references

- Warm cream / sand / paper-tinted portfolios — the AI-beige monoculture of 2025–2026.
- Generic SaaS landing page templates: hero metric grids, eyebrow-above-every-section, gradient text, identical card grids.
- Glassmorphism used decoratively.
- Portfolios that try too hard to be playful — emoji-heavy, illustrative, "whimsical."
- Anything that screams "I used a template."

## Design Principles

1. **Every pixel earns its place.** No decoration for its own sake. If removing something wouldn't hurt comprehension, remove it. Linear and Vercel set the bar: dense information with zero clutter.
2. **The craft IS the argument.** The site is itself a proof of skill. Typography, spacing, and motion should be so considered that a developer notices and respects it — even if they can't articulate why.
3. **Confidence, not performance.** Don't try to impress. State things plainly and let the work speak. Restraint signals more than spectacle.
4. **Motion serves clarity.** Animations orient, they don't entertain. Every transition has a job. Nothing bounces.
5. **System over surface.** Consistency in the small things (radius scale, spacing rhythm, color ramp discipline) signals the same thinking that produces good code.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target: WCAG AAA where achievable — minimum WCAG AA on all interactive surfaces. Concrete requirements:
- Body text contrast ≥ 7:1 against background (AAA).
- All interactive elements keyboard-navigable with visible focus rings.
- `prefers-reduced-motion` respected site-wide (already in global.css).
- No color as the sole carrier of information.
- Alt text on all meaningful images.
